## Overview

### Purpose of LinkedIn Organic

LinkedIn organic content serves three strategic goals:

1. **Awareness**: Build IAML's visibility among HR professionals
2. **Authority**: Establish thought leadership in employment law training
3. **Warm audience**: Create engaged connections who are receptive to outreach

### Key Principles

- **Value first**: Every post should teach, inform, or inspire—not sell
- **Consistency beats virality**: Regular posting compounds over time
- **Engagement is reciprocal**: Engage with others' content as much as you post
- **Personal > Corporate**: Mike's personal brand drives IAML's brand
- **Not salesy**: Promotional content is 20% max; value content is 80%

## Content Pillars

### Pillar 1: Employment Law Insights (40% of content)

**What it is**: Timely, practical insights on employment law topics

**Examples:**
- New NLRB rulings and what they mean for HR
- ADA/FMLA updates and practical implications
- Discrimination law trends
- State-specific developments (California, etc.)
- Wage and hour issues

**Why it works**: Demonstrates expertise, provides immediate value, positions IAML as the source

---

### Pillar 2: Practical HR Guidance (25% of content)

**What it is**: How-to content for handling specific situations

**Examples:**
- How to document a termination properly
- What to do when an employee requests an accommodation
- Red flags in a workplace investigation
- How to respond to a union organizing campaign

**Why it works**: Solves real problems, highly shareable, shows IAML understands the job

---

### Pillar 3: Industry Commentary & Opinions (15% of content)

**What it is**: Mike's perspective on HR trends, challenges, and developments

**Examples:**
- Opinion on a recent court decision
- Commentary on HR's evolving role
- Thoughts on training approaches (why deep > shallow)
- Observations from working with HR professionals

**Why it works**: Builds personal brand, creates engagement through opinions, differentiates

---

### Pillar 4: Social Proof & IAML Stories (15% of content)

**What it is**: Testimonials, program highlights, faculty features, attendee outcomes

**Examples:**
- Quote graphics from attendees
- Faculty spotlight posts
- Program outcome stories
- Behind-the-scenes from programs

**Why it works**: Builds credibility, gentle promotion, humanizes IAML

---

### Pillar 5: Program Announcements (5% of content)

**What it is**: Direct program promotion

**Examples:**
- New schedule announcement
- Registration opening
- Last few spots available

**Why it works**: Necessary for conversion, but must be limited to avoid being salesy

**Rule**: Never more than 1 promotional post per week

## Posting Cadence

### Weekly Schedule

**Target: 5 posts per week (weekdays)**

| Day | Post Type | Pillar |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Monday | Employment Law Insight | Pillar 1 |
| Tuesday | Practical HR Guidance | Pillar 2 |
| Wednesday | Industry Commentary | Pillar 3 |
| Thursday | Social Proof / Faculty | Pillar 4 |
| Friday | Lighter content or Pillar 1 | Varies |

### Best Posting Times

**Optimal**: 7:00-8:30 AM or 11:00 AM-12:00 PM (recipient's time zone)
**Good**: 5:00-6:00 PM

**Why morning works**: Professionals check LinkedIn early; algorithm favors early engagement

### Consistency Rules

- Post at the same general time each day (±1 hour)
- Never go more than 2 days without posting
- Plan content weekly to avoid gaps
- Batch content creation when possible

## Post Formats & Templates

### Format 1: The Insight Post

**Structure:**
- Hook (first line grabs attention)
- Context (1-2 lines of setup)
- Insight (the valuable point)
- Implication (what it means for the reader)
- CTA (optional: question or invitation to engage)

**Template:**

```
[Hook: Surprising fact, question, or statement]

[Context: Brief setup - 1-2 lines]

[Insight: The main point - 2-4 lines, can use bullets]

[Implication: What this means for HR professionals]

[Optional CTA: Question to drive comments]
```

**Example:**

```
Union election petitions doubled since 2021.

That's not a typo. The NLRB received 3,286 petitions in FY2024, up 27% from the prior year.

Here's what that means for HR:
→ Your union avoidance playbook from 5 years ago is outdated
→ The Cemex decision changed the rules on recognition demands
→ Managers need training on what they can and can't say

The companies getting organized aren't the ones with bad cultures. They're the ones who weren't prepared.

Is your team ready?
```

---

### Format 2: The Practical Tip

**Structure:**
- Problem statement (hook)
- The mistake most make
- The better approach
- How to implement

**Template:**

```
[Problem or situation most HR professionals face]

Most people handle it this way:
[Common approach]

Here's the problem with that:
[Why it's risky or suboptimal]

Instead, try this:
[Better approach - can use bullets or numbered list]

The difference is [outcome].
```

**Example:**

```
An employee asks for an ADA accommodation you've never heard of.

Most HR professionals panic and either:
a) Say yes immediately (creating precedent)
b) Say no reflexively (risking liability)

Both are wrong.

Here's what actually works:

1. Acknowledge the request in writing
2. Start the interactive process (it's required)
3. Ask for documentation of the disability
4. Explore reasonable accommodations together
5. Document everything

You don't have to say yes to every request. But you have to engage in good faith.

The documentation is your defense if it ever becomes a lawsuit.
```

**Structure:**
- Setup (what happened or what you observed)

**Template:**

HR professionals are expected to handle legal minefields with zero formal legal training.

And then we're surprised when things go wrong.

### Format 5: The List Post

**Structure:**

1. [Point 1]
2. [Point 2]
3. [Point 3]
4. [Point 4]

```

Every one of these is preventable with proper training and process.

What would you add?
```

## Engagement Strategy

### Why Engagement Matters

LinkedIn's algorithm rewards engagement. When you engage with others' content, you:
- Appear in more feeds
- Build relationships
- Increase likelihood they'll engage with your content
- Stay top of mind

### Engagement Time Investment

| Level | Time/Day | Activities |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Good** | 15 min | Like 5-10 posts, leave 3-5 short comments |
| **Better** | 30 min | Above + 2-3 thoughtful comments, reply to all comments on your posts |
| **Best** | 45-60 min | All above + proactive DMs to engaged connections, start conversations |

**Recommendation**: Start with "Good," scale to "Better" as you build habit.

### Who to Engage With

**Priority targets:**
1. HR professionals at target companies (ABM list)
2. HR influencers and thought leaders
3. Employment attorneys (potential faculty, referrers)
4. SHRM and HRCI-related accounts
5. Anyone who engages with your content

### How to Comment Effectively

**Bad comments** (avoid):
- "Great post!"
- "I agree"
- "Thanks for sharing"
- Emoji-only reactions

**Good comments** (aim for these):
- Add a perspective: "This reminds me of..."
- Ask a thoughtful question: "How would you handle this if..."
- Share an example: "We saw this with a client who..."
- Respectfully disagree: "Interesting point. I've seen it differently..."

**Format for good comments:**
1. Acknowledge their point (1 line)
2. Add your perspective or question (1-2 lines)
3. Optional: Invite further discussion

**Example:**

Their post: About ADA accommodation challenges

Your comment:
```
The interactive process piece is where most employers get tripped up. They either skip it or treat it as a formality.

In my experience, the companies that do it well document every conversation and explore multiple options—even ones they ultimately can't approve. That paper trail is the difference between winning and losing a lawsuit.

What do you find is the biggest challenge for HR in that process?
```

### Responding to Comments on Your Posts

- **Respond within 2-4 hours** if possible (algorithm boost)
- **Reply to every comment** in the first 24 hours
- **Ask follow-up questions** to keep the thread going
- **Tag people** when relevant to bring them into conversation

## Hashtag Strategy

### Approach

LinkedIn hashtags are less powerful than on other platforms, but they help with discoverability. Use them sparingly and strategically.

### Rules

- **3-5 hashtags per post** (at the end)
- **Mix of broad and niche**
- **Relevant to content** (not just popular)

### Recommended Hashtags

**Broad (use 1-2):**
- #HR
- #HumanResources
- #Leadership

**Mid-tier (use 1-2):**
- #EmploymentLaw
- #HRProfessionals
- #WorkplaceCulture
- #PeopleManagement

**Niche (use 1-2):**
- #EmployeeRelations
- #HRCompliance
- #LaborLaw
- #WorkplaceInvestigations
- #HRTraining

**Topic-specific (when relevant):**
- #FMLA
- #ADA

## Metrics & Optimization

### Key Metrics to Track

| Metric | What It Tells You | Good Benchmark |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Impressions** | How many people saw your content | Growing month-over-month |
| **Engagement rate** | (Likes + Comments + Shares) / Impressions | 2-5% |
| **Followers gained** | Growth from content | 50-100/month (early stage) |
| **Profile views** | Interest in you/IAML | Growing month-over-month |
| **Comments per post** | Engagement quality | 5+ average |
| **Shares** | Content resonance | Any shares are good |

### Monthly Review

Each month, review:
1. **Top 5 performing posts**: What worked? Topic, format, time?
2. **Bottom 5 performing posts**: What didn't work? Why?
3. **Follower growth**: Trending up?
4. **Engagement trends**: Improving?

### Optimization Actions

- **More of what works**: Double down on topics/formats that perform
- **Less of what doesn't**: Cut formats or topics that consistently underperform
- **Test new things**: Try one new format or topic per month
- **Adjust timing**: If certain days/times perform better, shift schedule
